The Carbon Cycle

Understanding Earth's Carbon Balance - From Natural Cycles to Human Impact

What is the Carbon Cycle?

The carbon cycle describes how carbon atoms move between the atmosphere, oceans, land, and living organisms. Carbon is the backbone of all organic molecules and is essential for life on Earth.

  • Carbon exists in multiple forms: CO₂, organic matter, carbonates
  • Moves between reservoirs (pools) through various processes (fluxes)
  • Has been relatively balanced for millions of years
  • Now disrupted by human activities

Key Terminology

  • Carbon Pool (Reservoir): A place where carbon is stored
  • Carbon Flux: Movement of carbon between pools
  • Carbon Source: Process that releases carbon to atmosphere
  • Carbon Sink: Process that removes carbon from atmosphere
  • Residence Time: Average time carbon stays in a pool
  • Gt C: Gigatonnes of carbon (1 billion metric tons)

Why It Matters

  • CO₂ is a greenhouse gas that traps heat
  • Pre-industrial CO₂: ~280 ppm
  • Current CO₂: ~420 ppm (50% increase!)
  • Causes global warming and climate change
  • Ocean acidification threatens marine life
  • Understanding the cycle helps us find solutions

⚡ Fast (Biological) Carbon Cycle

Timescale: Days to thousands of years

🌱

Photosynthesis

Plants, algae, and cyanobacteria absorb CO₂ and convert it to organic carbon. Removes ~120 Gt C/year from atmosphere.

Seconds to hours
🫁

Respiration

All organisms break down organic molecules for energy, releasing CO₂. Returns ~119 Gt C/year to atmosphere.

Continuous
🦠

Decomposition

Bacteria and fungi break down dead organic matter, releasing CO₂. Rate depends on temperature and moisture.

Days to decades
🌊

Ocean-Atmosphere Exchange

CO₂ dissolves in cold surface waters (sink) and releases from warm waters (source). Net absorption: ~2.5 Gt C/year.

Days to years
🔥

Wildfires

Combustion rapidly releases carbon stored in vegetation. Can release decades of accumulated carbon in hours.

Hours to days

🗻 Slow (Geological) Carbon Cycle

Timescale: Thousands to millions of years

🐚

Marine Carbonate Formation

Shell-building organisms (foraminifera, corals, mollusks) use dissolved carbon to make CaCO₃ shells. When they die, shells sink to seafloor.

Thousands of years
🪨

Lithification (Rock Formation)

Sediments compress into limestone and other carbonate rocks over millions of years. Locks carbon away for geological time.

Millions of years
🌋

Volcanic Outgassing

Volcanoes release CO₂ from subducted carbonate rocks and mantle. Returns ~0.1 Gt C/year to atmosphere - tiny but crucial over geological time.

Millions of years
ite

Weathering of Silicate Rocks

Chemical weathering consumes atmospheric CO₂: CO₂ + CaSiO₃ → CaCO₃ + SiO₂. Major long-term carbon sink.

Millions of years
⛽

Fossil Fuel Formation

Dead organisms buried before decomposition become coal, oil, gas under heat and pressure. Removes carbon for 100+ million years.

100+ million years

⚠️ The Problem: Humans Are Short-Circuiting the Slow Cycle

Fossil fuels represent carbon that was locked away over hundreds of millions of years. By burning them, we're releasing this ancient carbon back to the atmosphere in just ~200 years - about a million times faster than it was stored!

The fast carbon cycle cannot absorb this excess quickly enough. While oceans and forests do take up some of our emissions (about 50%), the rest accumulates in the atmosphere, causing warming.

FRQ 1. Explain how increased carbon dioxide concentrations in the atmosphere cause warming. In your response, describe the underlying physical mechanism (the greenhouse effect) and at least one feedback that amplifies the warming.

Model Answer
  1. Solar energy in: The Sun emits mostly shortwave radiation (visible/UV), which passes through the atmosphere and is absorbed by Earth's surface, warming it.
  2. Earth re-emits longwave radiation: The warm surface re-emits energy as longwave (infrared) radiation directed back out toward space.
  3. CO₂ absorbs infrared: CO₂ molecules have vibrational modes (bond stretching and bending) that resonate at infrared wavelengths, so they absorb outgoing IR photons rather than letting them escape.
  4. Re-radiation in all directions: Energized CO₂ molecules re-emit IR in all directions — including back down to the surface. This trapped energy raises the equilibrium temperature of the lower atmosphere and surface (the greenhouse effect).
  5. More CO₂ → more warming: Adding CO₂ thickens the IR-absorbing "blanket," so a larger fraction of outgoing infrared is intercepted. The surface must warm to a higher temperature to balance incoming solar energy.
  6. Positive feedbacks amplify the warming: Warmer air holds more water vapor (itself a powerful greenhouse gas → water-vapor feedback), and melting ice/snow exposes darker land and ocean (ice-albedo feedback), absorbing even more solar energy. These feedbacks roughly double the direct CO₂ warming.

Result: CO₂ rising from ~280 ppm pre-industrial to ~420 ppm today has driven about 1.2 °C of global warming.

FRQ 2. Explain how warming affects organisms and their ecological niches. Include at least three distinct mechanisms (e.g., range shifts, phenological mismatches, niche compression, mutualism disruption) and give a specific organism or ecosystem example for each.

Model Answer

Definition: An organism's ecological niche is the full set of biotic and abiotic conditions in which it can survive and reproduce — temperature range, moisture, food sources, breeding cues, and interactions with other species. Warming disrupts niches in several ways:

  1. Geographic range shifts: Species track their preferred climate by moving toward the poles or to higher elevations. Example: many North American tree species are migrating northward, and marine fish are shifting ranges by ~70 km per decade.
  2. Phenological mismatches: Life-cycle events (flowering, hatching, migration) are often triggered by temperature cues. When interacting species respond differently, their timing decouples. Example: pied flycatchers arrive at breeding sites after the peak caterpillar emergence has already passed, reducing chick survival.
  3. Niche compression and extinction: Mountaintop and polar species have nowhere cooler to retreat to — their niche literally disappears. Examples: American pikas going locally extinct on warming mountaintops; polar bears losing sea-ice hunting platforms.
  4. Ocean warming and acidification: Warmer water expels symbiotic zooxanthellae from corals (coral bleaching), and CO₂ dissolved as carbonic acid lowers ocean pH, dissolving CaCO₃ shells of mollusks, plankton, and corals — undermining whole marine food webs.
  5. Disrupted mutualisms: Pollinators and the plants they pollinate, or mycorrhizal fungi and tree roots, evolved under specific climate conditions. Warming can separate partners spatially or temporally, weakening both species.
  6. Winners and losers / community reshuffling: Generalists, invasive species, and disease vectors (mosquitoes carrying malaria, ticks carrying Lyme) often expand into newly suitable areas, while specialists decline. This restructures community composition and reduces overall biodiversity.

Big picture: Warming is reshuffling ecosystems faster than many species can evolve or migrate, producing range shifts, mismatches, and extinctions — a global reorganization of where organisms live and how they interact.
